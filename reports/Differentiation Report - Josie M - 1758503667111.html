Student Learning Profile Summary

Based on the provided information, Josie's profile presents a unique intersection of needs that requires a multifaceted approach. Her hearing impairment means she primarily accesses information visually and through amplification, making the auditory channel of traditional instruction a significant barrier (Easterbrooks & Beal-Alvarez, 2013). As an EAL learner at a beginner proficiency level, she is simultaneously acquiring academic content and the language of instruction, which doubles the cognitive load (Cummins, 2000). This combination often manifests as academic struggle, likely stemming from inaccessible instruction, language barriers, and potential social isolation.

  • Strengths to Leverage: High potential for visual learning, resilience, and likely a foundation in her first language (L1) that can be used as a bridge.

  • Primary Challenges: Limited access to auditory information, high cognitive load from dual-language processing, and potential gaps in foundational academic concepts due to these barriers.

  • Optimal Learning Conditions: A visually rich, language-focused environment with reduced auditory distractions. Instruction must be explicit, scaffolded, and provide multiple means for engagement, representation, and action & expression (CAST, 2018).

1. Content Modifications

Adjusting Complexity


  • Chunking with Visual Anchors: Break down multi-step tasks or dense texts into 3-4 step sequences. For each chunk, provide a corresponding icon or simple image.

  • *Example:* For a science lab, the worksheet has four pictures: 1. Beaker, 2. Thermometer, 3. Pencil writing on paper, 4. Magnifying glass. Each picture is numbered and has one sentence of instruction beside it.

  • *Research:* Supports working memory limitations common for EAL learners and reduces language processing load (Sweller, 2011).

  • Tiered Vocabulary Lists: Pre-teach 3-5 essential content-specific words (Tier 3) and 3-5 high-utility academic words (Tier 2) before a lesson. Provide images, definitions, and L1 translations (if possible and appropriate).

  • *Example:* For a history lesson on "expansion," pre-teach: *manifest destiny* (with a map image), *territory* (with an image of land), *acquire* (synonyms: get, obtain), *negotiate* (synonyms: discuss, deal).

  • *Research:* Direct vocabulary instruction is one of the most effective methods for improving comprehension for EAL students (August & Shanahan, 2006).

  • Sentence Frames & Starter Banks: Provide grammatically correct structures for responding to questions verbally or in writing.

  • *Example:* "The cause of ______ was ______." "I agree/disagree with ______ because ______." "The first step in solving this is ______."

  • *Materials:* Laminated strips or a poster on her desk.

  • *Research:* Scaffolds language production and allows students to focus on content rather than syntax (Gibbons, 2015).

Multiple Representations


  • Visual Supports: Use graphic organizers for every major concept. A flowchart for processes, a Venn diagram for comparisons, a T-chart for pros/cons.

  • *Materials:* Supply a folder of blank, commonly used organizers. Use online tools like Google Drawings or Lucidchart for digital versions.

  • Auditory Options (Modified): Since Josie has a hearing impairment, ensure all audio is:

1. Captioned: Use closed captioning on all videos (YouTube, Netflix, etc.).
2. Amplified & Clear: If she uses an FM system, ensure you are using the microphone correctly. Speak facing the class, clearly, and at a moderate pace.
3. Transcribed: Provide a written transcript of audio clips or teacher lectures. Tools like Otter.ai can generate live transcripts.
  • Kinesthetic Activities: Incorporate manipulatives and models. In math, use algebra tiles. In science, use physical models of cells. In ELA, use word cards to physically build sentences.

  • *Research:* Embodied cognition theory suggests physical action can support abstract learning (Glenberg, 2008).

Interest-Based Adaptations


  • Culturally Relevant Text Selection: Where possible, incorporate texts, examples, and problems that reflect diverse backgrounds and experiences.

  • Choice Menus: For project-based learning, provide a "menu" of options for demonstrating understanding of a standard.

  • *Example:* For a book report, options could include: a character Instagram profile, a diorama of a key scene, a written interview with the protagonist, or a movie trailer pitch.

  • *Materials:* A simple rubric that applies to all choices to ensure consistent assessment criteria.

2. Process Modifications

Instructional Delivery


  • Think-Aloud Modeling: Verbally model your cognitive process for solving a problem or analyzing a text. Make the invisible steps of expert thinking visible.

  • *Example:* "When I see this math word problem, the first thing I do is look for key words. I see 'total,' which makes me think I need to add. So I will identify the numbers I need to add together..."

  • *Research:* Effective for both EAL learners and students who benefit from explicit instruction (Fisher & Frey, 2008).

  • Pacing & Wait Time: After asking a question, institute a mandatory 10-15 second "think time." This gives Josie time to process the language, formulate a thought, and perhaps prepare to type or write a response.

  • Balance: Structure lessons to include short, explicit teacher-led instruction (10 mins), followed by guided collaborative practice (15 mins), and then independent/partner work (15 mins).

Scaffolding Techniques


  • Step-by-Step Checklists: For complex tasks like essays or science reports, provide a checklist of discrete, manageable steps. She can check off each step as she completes it.

  • Peer Support & "Welcome Buddies": Partner Josie with a kind, empathetic peer who can serve as a model for routines, help clarify directions, and be a first line of support. Rotate this buddy periodically. Crucially, the buddy is not a tutor but a facilitator.

  • Gesture and Visual Cuing: Use consistent gestures for common instructions (e.g., pointing to ear for "listen," hands together for "get into groups," open palm for "stop").

Technology Integration


  • Assistive Technology: Ensure Josie has consistent access to and training on any prescribed assistive listening devices (FM system).

  • Digital Tools:

  • Google Read & Write: Provides text-to-speech (to hear text read aloud), speech-to-text (to dictate responses), picture dictionaries, and vocabulary lists.

  • Microsoft Immersive Reader: Built into Word and OneNote, it can read text aloud, break it into syllables, highlight parts of speech, and offer picture definitions.

  • Translation Tools: Google Translate can be used strategically for key words and phrases to bridge L1 understanding. Use it as a dictionary, not for translating entire passages.

  • Accessibility Features: Enable closed captioning on all devices and software. Use high-contrast visual displays.
