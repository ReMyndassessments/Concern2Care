Use a highlighter to color-code the steps: yellow for setting up the problem, green for the multiplication of the ones place, blue for the tens place, etc. This visual scaffold makes a complex process manageable (Hudson & Miller, 2006).

  • Concrete Example: Strategic Number Choice: When assigning practice problems, pre-select numbers that minimize computational frustration and allow focus on the core concept. For example, when introducing long division, use problems with single-digit divisors that result in no remainders before progressing to more complex numbers.

  • Concrete Example: Vocabulary Cards: Create a personal math word wall for Noel. On one side of an index card, write the term (e.g., "quotient"); on the back, write a student-friendly definition and a visual example. He can build this set throughout the year (Marzano, 2004).

Multiple Representations


  • Visual: Use "Cubes, Pictures, Numbers" to solve word problems. Noel must show his work in three ways: 1) Using base-ten blocks (cubes), 2) Drawing a diagram or bar model (pictures), and 3) Writing the number sentence (numbers). 2. Process Modifications

Instructional Delivery


  • Chunking & Pacing: Use a visual timer (e.g., Time Timer) to break independent work into manageable 10-15 minute chunks. After each chunk, provide a brief "brain break" or a chance for Noel to check in with you or a peer.

  • Collaborative Balance: Implement "Think-Pair-Share" for problem-solving. This gives Noel individual think time (processing) before discussing with one partner (low-risk practice) and then sharing with the whole group.

Scaffolding Techniques


  • Step-by-Step Checklists: For multi-step problems (like long division or multi-step word problems), provide a laminated checklist. Noel can use a dry-erase marker to check off each step as he completes it, promoting independence and metacognition.

  • Think-Alouds: Explicitly model your problem-solving thought process. "When I see this word problem, the first thing I do is look for the question. I see it asks for the total cost. That tells me I probably need to add or multiply..."

Technology Integration


  • Assistive Technology: Introduce text-to-speech tools (like the built-in Immersive Reader in Microsoft products or on a tablet) for reading word problems. This removes decoding barriers and allows focus on the math.

  • Digital Tools: Use interactive virtual manipulatives, such as those on the National Library of Virtual Manipulatives or Math Learning Center apps, for hands-on practice without physical clutter.
